Saving on Fulldive -

making content aggregation less overwhelming


Fulldive is a content aggregator that organizes and curates content from all over. It's target audience includes people who are interested in taking control of and streamlining their content consumption. The old bookmarking and tagging features on our platform were lacking, exposing a general problem with content categorization. To address this need, I designed a new feature — Saving — on Fulldive.


With Saving, posts can be saved to specific tags. This lets people easily find their previously saved content and gives us data to categorize content.


ROLE

I led the design for this feature, collaborating with the co-founder, our design manager (Grant), and the CTO.


PROBLEMS

  1. The old bookmarking feature displayed all saved content on the same page, which made looking for old posts difficult.

  2. The old tag system wasn't being used, as users found no reason to be tagging a post. As our platform lacked a content creation aspect, people didn't have motivation to be tagging to get views.

  3. Since people weren't tagging, our content categorization wasn't granular enough. Users wanted to be able to discover and find content more specifically than what we were providing them.

GOALS

  1. Help people easily sort through their saved content

  2. Help people find more specific content

  3. Help the engineers get the information needed to sort and categorize specific content

Consolidating our bookmarks and tags system into one save feature.


RESEARCH

To see how we could better categorize content, I conducted competitive analysis on platforms such as Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, and Tumblr to see how they approached filtering mass amounts of content. I also talked to several people to understand how they usually searched for content.



ITERATION



Initial explorations for the save on feed flow.


Initial explorations for how combining bookmarks and tags would change existing tag and bookmark pages.


Revising the initial design

With a clear idea on higher-level decisions, I began getting into the details of the design. For saving on feed, I came up with a detailed flow flushing out mechanics to present to the engineers.


Flow for saving on feed with notes on functionality.


After showing these initial designs though, some key issues arose. I decided to rehaul my initial design. I initially prioritized minimizing steps in the flow, but in doing so made it more confusing for people.


The initial pull-up for Saving was confusing due to too many CTAs.


SOLUTION

Be explicit with the main action on each page

To solve for these problems, I made sure to be more explicit on the expected action for each screen. This meant separating out the flow for saving to a new tag and polishing up the writing on each screen to make sure actions were clear.



The second iteration involved more steps, but communicated the steps clearer. This made users feel more confident while going through the flow. Additionally, we anticipated that people wouldn't be going through the whole flow for saving to a new tag often, so it was alright if there were more screens.


Some key changes from the initial flow.


INTERACTION

Communicating design intention clearly

To better communicate the flow with engineers, I made some interactions on Principle.


Saving from the feed


Rules for naming tags

Along the way, I formulated a set of rules for tagging on the platform to minimize too many similarly named tags.


Interaction for how the platform treats various cases of naming. Tags don't have spaces and aren't case sensitive, so space and capitalization differences are ignored.


Unsaving from the video player

I also had to consider how Saving worked with other areas of the app, such as the video player.


The blue tags are ones the person has saved to a video. While a tap leads to the tag page, a long press allows them to unsave the video. If their's is the only tag of that name on the video, then the tag is completely removed. If others have tagged the video the same name, then the tag just fades color to gray.


RESULT

After much discussion between the co-founders, the design team, and the CTO, we came to a consensus on this design. In one last meeting, I presented the final flow and interactions and got the go-ahead to export all the assets to Zeplin where it's currently awaiting development.


Designing this feature challenged me to think broadly in terms of how the decisions we made influenced user behavior but also in detail in terms of the design itself. Talking to the CTO was helpful in understanding what sort of things engineers need to consider when developing. I'm excited to see where this goes once it's launched.
